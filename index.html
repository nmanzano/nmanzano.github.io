Tutti

Tutti is a chat client app, designed and developed in just one week due to the collaborative work between Adrain Thomas and myself. For our culminating project at Galvanize, Adrain and I chose to learn the react native framework even though it is outside of the core curriculum at Galvanize, as we felt we wanted to develop the mobile application for both iOS and Android, and we knew we would benefit from the challenge of building for both platforms.

Throughout the project, Adrain and I worked tirelessly for seven days. During this time, we reached MVP because of our ability to collaborate, encourage, and motivate each other through the roadblocks we encountered, and we learned how to rely on each other’s strengths, as we white boarded problems that individually we could not solve.

For example, in order to challenge ourselves and create an application we were proud of, in addition to learning react native, we also chose to learn firebase for our database and authentication. While able to create authentication without any issues, the database presented its own unique challenges. Specifically, we were able to push chat messages into the database by the fifth day but had not structured it to present the chats in order of delivery. With only thirty minutes left until presentations, Adrain and I went on a brainstorming walk to separate ourselves from the computer screen and troubleshoot our current situation. It was during this moment, we developed the concept of adding timestamps to the messages to organize the data. After quickly implementing this theory, we found our application was successful.

Midpoint

Friends for friends created midpoint.

The concept was developed from the question: “Where do you want to meet up?”

To create a more equitable solution to this age old question, Midpoint uses various friends’ locations to detect a point of equal radial distance to all parties using Google Maps.

At Galvanize, during this project, my team and I encountered many firsts. It was our first group project, which helped us develop our collaboration, management, and communication skills, and it was our first time creating an extensive project using Angular and PostgreSql while working with Google Maps, which challenged us and developed our skills in other frameworks.

Working with a team proved the most vital learning point of this project. My team consisted of five people, including myself. In order to work as if in a real production environment, we decided to delegate the work. However, in order to grow individually, we chose to work on things we needed development in instead of working towards each of our strengths, which proved an excellent opportunity for us to learn how to communicate our needs and troubleshoot issues as a team through paired programming and whiteboarding.

While working with a team, we also learned the value of collaboration in the first few days, as we ran into creative differences forcing us to distance from each other, which only resulted in an overlapping in our code. During this set back, we learned the value of collaboration to reach our goal by looking to strategies that a developing team had implemented--organizing meetings, limiting who was allowed to commit to the master branch, and communication. With our ability to manage and collaborate from within, we were efficient enough to finish the project an entire day ahead of schedule and earned MVP; most importantly though, we learned the value of teamwork, which has become an invaluable lesson I have taken into my other projects.

Remember Meow

Remember Meow was the first app that I built!

After attending Galvanize for four weeks, I developed Remember Meow in one week to demonstrate my mastery of HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and jQuery.

Given creative freedom to design and develop anything, naturally, my two passions came to mind--cats and games. Using my prior knowledge of HTML and CSS and the curriculum learned at Galvanize, I chose to demonstrate my new skills in developing with JavaScript and jQuery, so I decided to have most of my code reside within the JavaScript file.

To accomplish this, I wrote three functions to create the tiles of the board during loading, which proved challenging. However, through the process of trial and error, I cemented my understanding of how to use functions to call another function.

Upon reflection, I recall the various struggles I encountered with the project--having the tiles flip on a timer, saving player options when choosing a card, allowing a player to win. It was these hurdles, that made me appreciate all the games I played. It made me wonder how many details the player omits from their experience while playing a game in comparison to the reality of the developer’s time spent on preparing all aspects of the game to function seamlessly. It was with this that I began to take more pride in my work, as I carried over the same appreciation to my other projects.
